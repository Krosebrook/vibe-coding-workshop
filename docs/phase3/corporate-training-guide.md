# Corporate Training Guide

> How to package, sell, and deliver the Vibe Coding Workshop as a corporate training engagement for teams of 8–12.

---

## Overview

The corporate format adapts the one-day workshop for private company teams. Instead of a mixed-background cohort of individuals, you're working with colleagues who share a context (the same company, product, or problem domain). This makes it easier to create relevant examples — and harder to stay generic.

**Sweet spot:** Teams where non-technical members (designers, PMs, marketers, founders) want to prototype ideas faster without always asking engineering.

---

## Package Tiers

### Tier 1 — Team Intro (Half-Day, 4 hours)
- Covers: Phases 1 & 2 only (fundamentals + 2 projects)
- Capacity: 8–12 participants
- Format: In-person or virtual
- Deliverable: Each participant ships 1 deployed app
- Best for: Initial exposure, executive buy-in workshop

### Tier 2 — Team Workshop (Full Day, 8 hours)
- Covers: All 4 projects, same curriculum as public workshop
- Capacity: 8–12 participants
- Format: In-person preferred, virtual available
- Deliverable: Each participant ships 4 deployed apps
- Best for: Hands-on adoption, team upskilling

### Tier 3 — Team Intensive (2 Days)
- Day 1: Full Vibe Coding Workshop (4 projects)
- Day 2: Team builds a real internal tool together using vibe coding
- Capacity: 8–12 participants
- Format: In-person strongly recommended
- Deliverable: 4 personal apps + 1 shared team prototype
- Best for: Culture change, leadership teams, product teams

## What Makes Corporate Different

### 1. Customized Examples
Use the company's actual context in prompts. Instead of "build a mood tracker," it might be "build a status tracker for your sprint board." This makes the learning stick.

**Before first session:**
- 30-min pre-call with the team lead
- Collect 2–3 internal problem statements
- Adapt 2 of the 4 project prompts to their context

### 2. Shared Codebase Option
For Tier 3, the Day 2 team build creates a shared internal tool. This:
- Gives the team something they'll actually use
- Creates natural "champion" behavior after the workshop
- Demonstrates real-world vibe coding workflow

### 3. No Public Portfolio Pressure
Individual participants aren't "shipping to the public" the way individuals are. Frame the Ship It moment differently:
- "You're shipping a proof of concept for [Company Name]"
- Invite a senior stakeholder to the Ship It ceremony

### 4. IT/Security Constraints
Large companies may restrict tool installs. Plan for this:
- Get approval for Cursor and VS Code installs 2 weeks before
- Confirm browser-based Claude access works on company WiFi
- Identify the IT contact who can unblock tools if needed
- Have a browser-only fallback plan (Claude + CodePen)

## Pre-Engagement Checklist

### 6 Weeks Before

- [ ] Discovery call with team lead / champion
  - What problem does the team want to solve?
  - What's the technical comfort level of participants?
  - Any IT/security restrictions?
  - Preferred format (in-person vs. virtual)?
  - Day 2 project idea (for Tier 3)?
- [ ] Confirm date, venue, and capacity
- [ ] Draft customized project prompts (based on their context)
- [ ] Send engagement letter / SOW

### 4 Weeks Before

- [ ] Send participant pre-work email
  - Accounts to create (GitHub, Cursor, Claude, Vercel)
  - The "one sentence idea" prompt
  - What a productive mindset looks like for the day
- [ ] Confirm IT approval for tools
- [ ] Customize 2 project templates using company context

### 2 Weeks Before

- [ ] Optional: 15-min participant tech check (virtual cohorts only)
- [ ] Finalize Day 2 project brief (Tier 3 only)
- [ ] Confirm headcount for catering (in-person only)

### 1 Week Before

- [ ] Final logistics email to all participants
- [ ] Review customized project prompts with team lead
- [ ] Ship printed materials if in-person

## Day-Of Adaptations

### Welcome — Corporate Version

Replace the general intro with:

> "Today you're not here to become a developer. You're here to understand what's possible — and to have proof that you can prototype ideas yourself. Everything you build today is yours. Your apps are real. Your URLs are live."

### Project Customization Examples

| Standard Template | Corporate Adaptation Example |
|-------------------|------------------------------|
| Personal Landing Page | Team member's professional profile or role intro page |
| Mood Tracker | Sprint retrospective tracker or weekly team pulse |
| Recipe Finder | Internal knowledge base search / FAQ finder |
| AI Chat Assistant | Customer FAQ bot or internal process assistant |

### Ship It — Corporate Version

- Invite team lead / manager to join the last 30 minutes
- Have participants share their live app URL in the team Slack channel
- Frame it: "This is what your team can now prototype in a day."

## Post-Engagement

### Same Day
- [ ] Send recap email with all live URLs
- [ ] Share recording (virtual format)
- [ ] Feedback form (NPS + qualitative)

### 1 Week After
- [ ] Check-in email: "What did you build since the workshop?"
- [ ] Share one follow-up resource (prompt playbook or G24)
- [ ] Ask the team lead: "What would a Day 2 project look like?"

### 1 Month After
- [ ] Impact check-in: "What have your team members built or prototyped?"
- [ ] Referral ask: "Know another team that could benefit?"
- [ ] Follow-up engagement discussion

## Sales Process

1. **Inbound** — Corporate inquiries come via hello@vibecodingworkshop.com
2. **Discovery call** — 30 min, understand the need and fit
3. **Proposal** — Send within 48 hours, including:
   - Which tier is right for them
   - What customization looks like
   - Timeline and logistics
   - Pricing
4. **SOW / Contract** — Simple agreement, 50% deposit to hold the date
5. **Pre-work** — Follow the 6-week checklist above
6. **Deliver** — Day-of using this guide
7. **Follow-up** — Use post-engagement sequence

---

## Ideal Corporate Client Profile

Best results when the team has:
- At least 1 internal champion who attended a public workshop
- Mix of technical and non-technical participants
- A real problem they want to prototype
- Management support for experimentation

Watch out for:
- Teams where IT will block every tool
- Participants who were told to attend (not self-selected)
- Unrealistic expectations: "We want everyone to be a developer after this"
